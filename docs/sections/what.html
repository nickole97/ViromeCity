What is a virus?

The smallest thing you'll spend this whole guide chasing.

Viruses are the smallest known packages of life — barely alive, arguably not — yet the most abundant on the planet.

A virus has a relatively simple structure: essentially, genetic material wrapped in a protein shell. The capsid is that shell, and it protects the genome inside. The genome can come in different variants — RNA or DNA, single- or double-stranded. Some viruses also carry an outer lipid envelope studded with surface proteins that help it attach to host cells (like the spikes of a coronavirus) — but many are non-enveloped.

Fivi: I'm just a genome in a shell. Learn my parts and you'll know how to catch me.

the parts (3 core + 1 optional)

  • 1Genome — the nucleic acid (DNA or RNA); the instructions you sequence.
  • 2Capsid — the protein shell that packages and protects the genome.
  • 3Surface / attachment proteins — how it recognizes and enters a host.
  • +4Envelope (optional) — a lipid membrane borrowed from the host; only some viruses have one.

the classification — three ways to read a virus

We're not one thing. There are several ways to make sense of a virus — three main axes. A given virus has all three at once: a host, a genome type, and a state.

Axis 1 · Host — who we infect (the main one)

HOST·1 infects: bacteria

Bacteriophage

Infects bacteria, never you. It is the most abundant member of the virome and dominates stool and saliva. Its lunar-lander shape injects its genome into the bacterium.

HOST·2 infects: eukaryotic cells

Eukaryotic virus

Infects eukaryotic cells. Home to both commensals and classic pathogens: herpes, papilloma, and anelloviruses (TTV, ~70% of the plasma virome).

HOST·3 infects: archaea

Archaeal virus

Infects archaea, microbes distinct from bacteria. Rare, understudied, and with striking shapes like this lemon form.
